English

There is a serious discrepancy in the cell viability data presented for quercetin (Que) treatment in Figure 4B and Figure S3D. The data points and bar graphs indicate that viability in the "TAC + Que" group appears lower than in the "TAC" group, which contradicts the textual claim that Que is protective. Because this inconsistency appears in both main and supplementary figures, the reviewer requests urgent clarification and correction.

2. Response to Reviewer

We sincerely thank the reviewer for identifying this critical inconsistency. We fully agree that any mismatch between plotted group order and textual interpretation can undermine the scientific credibility of the conclusion. After re-checking the source datasets and plotting settings, we confirmed that the discrepancy originated from incorrect graph-display mapping during figure preparation, rather than from the underlying numerical data table. We have therefore corrected the affected panels and re-verified consistency between the source data tables and displayed plots. In the revised manuscript, we have updated the corresponding figure presentation and revised the related text to ensure strict alignment between data visualization and interpretation. We also clarified the biological conclusion more explicitly: under TAC-induced injury, quercetin shows a protective effect in wild-type cells, whereas this effect is markedly attenuated after Prdx1 knockdown, supporting a Prdx1-dependent mechanism. To avoid ambiguity, we carefully rechecked all relevant figure citations and statements associated with these results in both the main text and supplementary section. We appreciate this comment, which substantially improved the rigor and clarity of our data presentation.

3. Revised Manuscript Excerpt (Clean Version)

As described above, a dual-targeting drug delivery strategy was required to address the functional contradiction of Prdx1 in SAP, namely promoting intracellular Prdx1 activity while neutralizing extracellular Prdx1. Through literature review and pharmacological screening, we identified quercetin (Que), a natural flavonoid, as a potential regulator of intracellular Prdx1 with antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. As shown in Figure 4A, Que improved the viability of MPC-83 cells under taurocholate acid (TAC)-induced injury. In contrast, this protective effect was markedly attenuated in Prdx1-knockdown cells (Figure 4B), indicating that the efficacy of Que is Prdx1-dependent. Flow cytometry and BODIPY 581/591 staining further supported that Prdx1 deficiency weakened the protective effects of Que (Figure 4C-D). In addition, Que intervention did not significantly alter Prdx1 mRNA or total protein levels (Figure S3A and Figure S3B), and no evident changes were observed in potential upstream regulators, including HIF-1α, NRF2, and SP1 (Figure S3C). Together, these data suggest that Que mainly regulates Prdx1 at the post-translational level rather than by changing its expression. However, the low solubility and bioavailability of Que limit its direct application. Given evidence that extracellular vesicles (EVs) can improve the loading efficiency and cellular internalization of poorly soluble compounds, we isolated EVs from HEK-293T cells and loaded them with Que. Co-immunoprecipitation results demonstrated that EV@Que enhanced Prdx1 acetylation more effectively than free Que (Figure 4G), without changing total Prdx1 protein expression. Consistently, under TAC pretreatment, EV@Que provided greater protection of MPC-83 cell viability than free Que alone (Figure S3D), likely due to improved intracellular delivery of Que by EVs.
